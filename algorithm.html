⚙️ CHRONOLOGY OF ALGORITHMS

from ancient clay tablets to neural networks — the complete historical timeline, arranged in strict chronological order.

📜 ANTIQUITY · before 500 CE

~1800 BCE

Babylonian division & root extraction

Clay tablet YBC 7289 shows the square root of 2 computed with astonishing accuracy using an iterative method equivalent to Heron's algorithm. Oldest known numerical algorithm.

~300 BCE

Euclid's algorithm (greatest common divisor)

Euclid's Elements (Book VII) describes the subtraction‑based method for GCD — the oldest algorithm still in common use. Pure recursive logic without computers.

~250 BCE

Sieve of Eratosthenes

Eratosthenes of Cyrene invents a filtering algorithm to find all prime numbers up to a given limit. First example of a sieve / space‑time tradeoff.

🕯️ MEDIEVAL & ISLAMIC GOLDEN AGE · 500–1500

~825

Al‑Khwarizmi & systematic algebra

Persian mathematician Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al‑Khwārizmī writes "Al‑Jabr", from which the word algorithm derives. Describes step‑by‑step methods for solving linear and quadratic equations.

~1202

Fibonacci's Liber Abaci

Leonardo of Pisa introduces Hindu‑Arabic numerals to Europe and describes algorithms for multiplication, division, and the Fibonacci sequence (though known earlier in India).

RENAISSANCE · 1500–1800

1614

Napier's logarithms

John Napier publishes tables of logarithms, a computational algorithm that transforms multiplication into addition — the first hardware acceleration (paper + tables) for arithmetic.

1671–76

Leibniz & binary arithmetic

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz develops the binary system and describes a mechanical calculating machine — the first stored‑program concept, later fundamental to all computer algorithms.

🧮 19th CENTURY · algorithm theory emerges

1805

Legendre / Gauss least squares

Adrien‑Marie Legendre and Carl Friedrich Gauss independently publish the method of least squares — the first statistical parameter‑estimation algorithm.

1837

Babbage & the Analytical Engine

Charles Babbage designs the first Turing‑complete mechanical computer. Ada Lovelace writes a "plan" to compute Bernoulli numbers — widely considered the first algorithm intended for a machine.

1843

Gauss–Seidel iteration

Iterative method for solving linear systems, used by Gauss in letters and later formalized by Seidel. Essential for numerical algorithms.

1890

Hollerith tabulator

Herman Hollerith's punched‑card sorting algorithm for the US census — first large‑scale data‑processing algorithm.

1900–1940 · pre‑electronic algorithms

1913

Runge–Kutta methods

Carl Runge and Martin Kutta develop explicit numerical methods for solving ordinary differential equations — still the backbone of simulation.

1928

Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy (CFL) condition

Stability criterion for numerical solutions of partial differential equations — foundational for computational fluid dynamics algorithms.

1936

Turing machine & lambda calculus

Alan Turing ("On Computable Numbers") formalizes the concept of an algorithm via Turing machines. Alonzo Church simultaneously develops lambda calculus — the two models define algorithmic computability.

💡 1940–1960 · first electronic algorithms

1945

von Neumann architecture & merge sort

John von Neumann writes the "First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC", describing stored‑program concept. He also invents merge sort (one of the first O(n log n) sorts).

1947

Monte Carlo method

Stanisław Ulam, John von Neumann and Nicholas Metropolis develop statistical sampling algorithms at Los Alamos — named after Monte Carlo casino.

1951

Simplex algorithm

George Dantzig publishes the simplex method for linear programming — one of the most influential optimization algorithms.

1954

Dijkstra's shortest path (first conception)

Edsger W. Dijkstra begins thinking about the shortest‑path problem while grocery shopping with his fiancée. He publishes the algorithm in 1959.

1957

Bellman–Ford algorithm

Richard Bellman and Lester Ford Jr. independently derive the dynamic‑programming algorithm for shortest paths with negative edges.

⚙️ 1960–1970 · complexity & classic data structures

1960

Quicksort (C. A. R. Hoare)

Tony Hoare develops quicksort while working on machine translation at Moscow State University. Still the most widely used sorting algorithm.

1962

Red–black tree (Bayer) · hash table (Luhn)

Rudolf Bayer invents red–black trees (then called symmetric binary B‑trees). Hans Peter Luhn had already used hash tables in 1953, but they become widespread in the 1960s.

1964

Heap sort (Williams & Floyd)

J. W. J. Williams invents heap sort; Robert Floyd improves it. First O(n log n) in‑place sort.

1965

Fast Fourier Transform (Cooley–Tukey)

James Cooley and John Tukey publish the FFT algorithm, revolutionizing digital signal processing — though Gauss had sketched a similar method in 1805.

1968

Knuth's The Art of Computer Programming

First volumes of Donald Knuth's encyclopedic work, which formally defines and analyzes hundreds of algorithms and introduces asymptotic notation (Big O).

1970–1980 · NP‑completeness & public‑key

1971

Cook–Levin theorem (NP‑completeness)

Stephen Cook and Leonid Levin independently prove that the Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT) is NP‑complete — foundational for complexity theory.

1975

Genetic algorithms (Holland)

John Holland introduces genetic algorithms, inspired by natural selection — first major evolutionary computing paradigm.

1976

Knuth–Morris–Pratt string search

Donald Knuth, James H. Morris, and Vaughan Pratt develop the linear‑time string‑matching algorithm (KMP).

1977

RSA cryptosystem (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman)

First practical public‑key cryptosystem, based on the difficulty of factoring large integers. Revolutionizes secure communication.

1979

Dijkstra–Scholten algorithm · Paxos (Lamport)

Leslie Lamport begins work on the Paxos consensus algorithm (published 1989) — fundamental for distributed systems.

🔄 1980–1990 · randomization & parallelism

1983

Karmarkar's interior‑point method

Narendra Karmarkar presents a polynomial‑time linear‑programming algorithm, competitive with simplex for large problems.

1984

Fisher–Yates shuffle (modern version)

Richard Durstenfeld adapts the Fisher–Yates shuffle into the efficient O(n) algorithm used everywhere today.

1987

Backpropagation (Rumelhart, Hinton, Williams)

Popularisation of the backpropagation algorithm for training multilayer neural networks — ignites the first neural‑net revival.

🌐 1990–2000 · web & massive data

1991

PageRank (Brin & Page)

Larry Page and Sergey Brin develop PageRank at Stanford, the algorithm that powers the Google search engine — eigenvector centrality applied to hyperlinks.

1992

UTF‑8 (Thompson & Pike)

Ken Thompson and Rob Pike design UTF‑8, a variable‑width encoding algorithm that became the dominant character encoding for the web.

1994

SHA‑1 (NIST)

Secure Hash Algorithm 1 designed by NSA — widely used in cryptography and integrity verification until deprecated.

1996

Apache Lucene / full‑text indexing

Doug Cutting starts Lucene, implementing inverted‑index search algorithms that became the core of Elasticsearch and Solr.

2000–2010 · modern ML & large graphs

2001

Random forests (Breiman)

Leo Breiman publishes random forests, combining bagging and random feature selection — one of the most accurate off‑the‑shelf algorithms.

2004

MapReduce (Google)

Jeffrey Dean and Sanjay Ghemawat describe MapReduce — a programming model for processing huge datasets on clusters, inspiring Hadoop.

2006

Deep belief nets (Hinton) · SIFT (Lowe)

Geoffrey Hinton introduces fast learning algorithms for deep belief networks — starting the deep‑learning revolution. David Lowe's SIFT becomes the iconic feature‑detection algorithm in computer vision.

2008

Bitcoin/blockchain (Nakamoto)

Satoshi Nakamoto's Bitcoin whitepaper introduces the Nakamoto consensus algorithm (Proof‑of‑Work) — first practical decentralized cryptocurrency.

🧠 2010–2020 · transformers & reinforcement learning

2012

AlexNet (Krizhevsky, Sutskever, Hinton)

Breakthrough CNN architecture using ReLU and GPU‑accelerated backpropagation, winning ImageNet by a huge margin — ignites deep‑learning explosion.

2015

ResNet (He et al.)

Kaiming He et al. introduce residual learning, enabling training of ultra‑deep networks (152 layers) via skip connections. Fundamental to nearly all modern CV algorithms.

2017

Transformer (Vaswani et al.)

"Attention is all you need" — the Transformer architecture replaces RNNs with self‑attention, forming the backbone of BERT, GPT, and all modern large language models.

2018

BERT (Devlin et al.)

Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers sets new standards in NLP; pre‑training + fine‑tuning paradigm dominates.

🚀 2020–present · large‑scale foundation models

2020

GPT‑3 (Brown et al.)

175‑billion‑parameter language model, demonstrating few‑shot learning and sparking the generative AI revolution.

2022

ChatGPT & RLHF

OpenAI aligns large models with human feedback using reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) — algorithms that fine‑tune language models via preference comparisons.

2023

AlphaGeometry (DeepMind)

Neuro‑symbolic algorithm that solves olympiad‑level geometry problems, combining a neural language model with a symbolic deduction engine.
